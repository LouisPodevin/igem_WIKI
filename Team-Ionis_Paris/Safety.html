In the Laboratory

The BacTail project is part of the iGEM 2020 synthetic biology competition. BacTail is a tailor-made approach to specifically recognise, bind and lyse a pathogenic bacteria thanks to genetically engineered bacteria (more info on our wiki). 

When talking about synthetic biology, it is important that the safety considerations are reasonably anticipated. To do so, we first needed to ensure that policies and regulations in respect of environmental aspects were followed.

As a European Team based in Paris, we carefully researched the legal frame regarding the practice of synthetic biology in our country. In France, the main legal frame regarding Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs) is given by the European Union (EU). According to the Directive 2001/18/EC and Regulation 1829/2003, it is prohibited to use or manufacture a GMO without prior specific authorization. Applications for authorization of GMOs fall into three main categories, “contained use”, “use in deliberate release” and “deliberate release”1. 

Our project BacTail, as part of the iGEM competition 2020, falls into the category “contained use”, where GMOs can be used for research purposes. Our system is still in the proof of concept phase, therefore with respect to the regulations, all manipulations with the genetically engineered material were safely performed in our class 1 laboratory and were not intended to leave it. Concerning the outside world, our design was carefully thought through, i.e. at last, a self-deletion system that will be activated once the chassis is effective and has performed its mission whether in vitro or in vivo. Thanks to this system, the modified organism will never spread out of its site of action or be in an environment where it should not be.

Our Project

All team members that performed experiments in the laboratory are part of the Engineering School Sup’Biotech, and as students from this University, we received laboratory training sessions during our 1st, 2nd and 3rd years of studies. Thanks to these training sessions all lab members are aware of the good laboratory practices.

Furthermore, before entering the laboratory, everyone had to read a “Good Practice Guide for R&D Projects” which are specific safety and security guidelines given by our laboratory principle.

Some examples of safety applications in the laboratory: during the experiments everyone made sure to wear the appropriate safety equipment when needed (lab coat, gloves, protection glasses). In respect of the outside world, the waste was managed carefully. All biological material was carefully placed in yellow "DASRI" (Infectious Medical Waste Activities) bins specific for biological matters. Additionally, liquid waste containing modified bacteria was omitted to a 24-hour bleach treatment before being taken care of. 

All of this put together demonstrates that the laboratory security and safety measures were seriously taken into consideration by the Ionis Paris team 2020.

Beyond the Laboratory | Human Practices

The main concern raised during our Human Practices was privacy. Therefore, every time we conducted an interview or a presentation, we asked people we interviewed if they consent to put on their webcam or if they agree to take a picture at the end and if we can use their information or picture for our project. For this we had a written agreement according to the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). For example, one survey was organized, where it was guaranteed to anonymize the participants and we asked their consent to collect their answers for our project. 

Another concern was ensuring to deliver non oriented data by talking with as many different stakeholders that could be linked to our project. It was important for us to hear many different points of view. Therefore we contacted a large diversity of people from different activity sectors. However, it is important to note that our statistics and conclusions have to be analysed carefully.
