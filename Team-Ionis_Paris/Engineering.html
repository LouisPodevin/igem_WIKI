Engineering success

iGEM’s 2020 competition was a particular year. Given the public health situation, our team faced unexpected circumstances, both positive and negative.
For one, we consider ourselves lucky to have been able to enter a laboratory for a while. Still, we experienced considerable delays in the reception of our consumables or synthetized DNA fragments, which slowed BacTail’s iGEM engineering circle drastically.

However, due to the lock-down, we had the opportunity to exchange with other teams more frequently, which resulted in an unprecedented collaboration with an iGEM Team from India(IISER TIRUPATI) for the phase 3 of our BacTail project.

For more information of our project: Design Phase 1, Design Phase 2, Desgin Phase 3 and Results.

Notes

Chassis: In synthetic biology, chassis are cells which genetic systems can be used for its basic metabolic pathway to produce desired biological compounds. They should be capable of essential chemical transformations, such as the production of energy and co-factor regeneration.

E. coli: Escherichia coli is a Gram-negative bacteria, meaning that its cell wall is made of peptidoglycan. In average, E. coli’s are about 2.0 μm long and 0.25–1.0 μm in diameter.

FITC: Fluorescein isothiocyanate has excitation and emission spectrum peak wavelengths of approximately 495 nm/519 nm.

Gp37: Gene product 37 is the most distal region of the Long Tail Fiber of the T4 bacteriophage.

Lpp: E. coli’s lipoprotein is a alpha-helical protein and numerically the most abundant protein in E. coli, with more than 1 million copies per cell.

LTF: Bacteriophage T4 Long Tail Fibers are composed of four different proteins (gp 34, gp35, gp36, and gp37) and are segments of approximately 70 nm.13

Lpp-Ompa: The co-expressed Lpp-OmpA genes include a lipoprotein signal peptide and the first nine N-terminal amino acids of the E. coli lipoprotein (Lpp) attached to a transmembrane domain (amino acids 46–159) from outer membrane protein A (OmpA).3

OmpA: Outer membrane protein A family is a group of surface-exposed, porin proteins and its molecular mass is between 28 kDa to 36 kDa11.

PCR: Polymerase chain reaction is a method used to rapidly make millions to billions of copies of a specific DNA sample.

RBP: Receptor Binding Protein, concerning our topic the gp37 is part of the RBPs of the T4 bacteriophage’s LTFs.

TEV site: The TEV cut site is the sequence as follows: ENLYFQ\(G/S) (where “\” denotes the cleaved site) and is a highly specific sequence of the cysteine protease from Tobacco Etch Virus (TEV).

Phase 1 | Targeting and binding to the target bacteria

Research

The first phase of our project consists in specifically recognizing and binding a target bacterium. Thus, our very priority was to find a system that would grant recognition and specific binding to target bacteria which are resistant to antibiotics.

One of our teammates came up with an idea: why not exploit the recognition abilities of the "natural" predators of bacteria, which are bacteriophages? Rapidly, as a team, we tried to identified which exact region was responsible for the bacteriophage binding.

This is where our project BacTail begun.

Organization – Journal club sessions

From this point on, many questions arose that needed answers and required a substantial amount of research. Being confined to our respective homes, we started to organize weekly virtual "journal club" sessions. These sessions entailed presenting scientific articles that seemed relevant for the questions we each had. Thanks to these sessions, we were able to start debates and take decisions during the research phase. The questions and topics we encountered are as follows:

Which is the exact region that is responsible for the specific binding of a bacteriophage to its target bacterium?

The first information that came up was that the tip of the long tail fibers (LTF) of a certain class of bacteriophages recognize specific sites on the outer membranes of bacteria13. Thus, our goal was to design a system which function is to export the distal tip of a phage receptor binding protein (RBP) on the outside membrane of our future chassis.

Can we use the distal tip of a phage receptor binding protein on its own?

Once we had found the T4 RBP, specific for recognition, we had to measure the feasibility of using the RBP of bacteriophages on its own. In fact, the article published in 2019 by Dunne et al. showed that the recognition and binding of Salmonella cells could be accomplished by only using the LTF of the S16 phage, the natural predator of Salmonella1. More information here: Design PHASE 1.

Eventually, the experiments conducted by Dunne et al. successfully proved that the unique gp37-gp38 protein of the S16 phage is sufficient to bind the target Salmonella cell. Therefore, we decided to use these receptor binding proteins in our BioBrick to specifically recognize and bind a target bacterium.

How do we export the receptor binding protein at the the surface of our chassis?

For the exportation system, we found the part BBa_K1694002 designed by iGEM15_NCTU-Formosa and encoding for the Lpp-OmpA expression system3.

Seeking the appropriate chassis

It demanded a considerable amount of research to decide which target and chassis to use for our project. The only information we had was that, for safety issues, we were limited to class 1 micro-organism. For the chassis, we chose an Escherichia coli (E.coli) K12 since it is a well-studied benign laboratory model organism2 and performing experiments, such as transformations, would be easier.

Seeking the appropriate target

For the target, we first needed to evaluate the already characterized bacteriophages in literature. Many host-associated receptors targeted by bacteriophages have been researched4,5,6,7,8,9. In Bertozzi et al.10, several tables compiles the receptors that have been identified for Gram-positive and –negative bacteria.

At first, we considered taking Bacillus subtilis (Gram-positive) as our target.
However, gram-positive bacteria have more complex and denser cell walls. According to Bertozzi et al., this adds some difficulty in the characterization of the phage receptor's specificity. Additionally, it seemed that the T4 coliphage was a more studied bacteriophage12. Therefore, for our proof of concept, we decided to target an E. coli as well. How will we be able to validate the expression of the protein?

Finding how to express the gp37 protein on the surface of our chassis was one challenge but validating its expression and its specific localization was a real obstacle.
Adding a fluorescence marker, a red fluorescence protein, was a rudimentary idea to localize the protein or any expression at all, around the bacteria thanks to microscopy.
Additionally, we wanted to be able to cut the protein once expressed on the outer membrane of our chassis and purify the expressed protein. Therefore, we added a cleavage site, the TEV cleavage site, which is a unique cleavage site of the cysteine protease from the Tobacco Etch Virus (TEV)13. This cleavage site is followed by a His-tag for the purification step which can be performed with Ni2+ chromatography columns14 , see on Figure 3.

Test

Once our fragments arrived, we tried to assemble them via Hifi Assembly technique to obtain the BioBrick 1 as depictured in Figure 5, more information on this on the Phase 1 Results page. However, with this BioBrick design we faced different issues.

Problem 1 - Our plasmid backbones

We had 4 fragments to assemble, which made it a little bit difficult in the short time we had. On top of that, the plasmid backbones we had in our possession, already contained the RFP, between the prefix and suffix region.
Even only after amplifying the backbone via PCR, we still obtained too many red colonies with our ligation control (cut plasmid with no insert but ligase) after transformation.

Problem 2 – The verification step

Additionally, for the verification step, we were supposed to reveal the presence of the BioBrick on the surface of our bacteria with microscopy thanks to the presence of the RFP. However, the microscope we needed was not available on our campus at that time. Therefore, we needed to urgently adapt our construction to a new validation technique.

Learn / Improve

Solutions for Problem 1 - Our plasmid backbones

Solution A: Addition of Alkaline phosphatase

Regarding the plasmid, we obtained too many colonies on the control plates with the uncut plasmid backbone only.
Our hypothesis is that the plasmid would re-ligate itself instead of ligate with the inserts. That is why, after some research we realized that this self-ligation process could be enhanced by the presence of phosphate at the 5' ends of the digested plasmid.
In a scientific article, we found that adding Alkaline phosphatase15, after having performed the restriction enzyme digestion, would remove the phosphates group located on the 5' end of the plasmid DNA and thus prevent self-ligation.

Solution B: Addition of DpnI

Furthermore, another hypothesis is that some plasmids that were used as DNA template for our PCR remained intact in our samples until the transformation step.
In that case they would still possess the RFP gene, leading to red transformed bacteria.

DpnI is naturally present in bacteria16 and is naturally used as a defense system allowing the bacterium to defend itself against bacteriophages that infect it by inserting their viral DNA .
This DNA is double stranded and methylated while the bacterial DNA is non-methylated.
This is why it has the ability to recognize and cleave only when its recognition site is methylated and therefore belongs to the bacteriophage.
In our project, the DpnI enzyme will be used on the plasmid after the PCR step, in order to destroy the original template plasmid that has not been amplified.

We did the test and amplified two pSB1A3 plasmids with the same primers by PCR.
One was treated with DpnI and one not. Both were transformed in bacteria and the next day a major difference between them was observable, see Figure 7.

Solution for Problem 2 – The verification step

By talking to some of our professors (see Attributions, section laboratory), we decided to adjust our BioBrick design.
Instead of using the red fluorescence marker to validate the presence of our BioBrick, we decided to directly target the His-tag with a primary monoclonal anti-his antibody conjugated to a fluorescence marker that emits wavelength (517 nm) detectable with a fluorescence spectrophotometer.

Test

To verify if our bacteria have correctly exported the receptor binding protein gp37 on their outer membrane, we established an immunofluorescence labelling protocol.
The aim of this protocol is to target bacteria in a liquid culture with an anti-his antibody conjugated to a FITC fluorophore.
This liquid culture will then be placed into 96-well plates to be put into a fluorescence plate reader.
