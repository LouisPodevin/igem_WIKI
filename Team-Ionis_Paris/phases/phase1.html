Introduction
Phase 1 is the recognition of the target. For this we were inspired by the characteristics of bacteriophages which allow them to specifically bind to their host. A bacteriophage is a virus which infects a specific bacterium leading to its lysis. Thus, bacteriophages are the natural predators of bacteria. Thousands of them exist in nature and every day, new ones are found. There are 10 different bacteriophage families according to morphology1. For example, the families belonging to the Caudovirales order are Myoviridae, Siiphoviridae or Podoviridae. A phage will specifically bind to different bacterial receptors according to the architecture of the target cell wall.
For Gram positive bacteria, the binding motifs are found in peptidoglycan, teichoic acids, lipoteichoic acids or proteins. For Gram negative bacteria, the phage will bind to the liposaccharides (LPS) or outer membrane proteins (Omp). The specific binding of the phage to the target cell triggers the injection of its genetic material. This leads to a multiplication of the phage inside the host, which ends up lysing(lytic bacteriophages).
To better understand the mechanism of bacteriophage infection, will be taking the example of the structure of the Myoviridae family which has the particularity to possessLong Tail Fibers (LTFs). These LTFs are attached to the phages base plate and play a role in phage adsorption to the target thanks to adhesins. In fact, they are responsible for the specific and reversible interaction with the receptors on the host membrane and once bound, they induce a conformational change. This brings the tail spikes near the target with which they irreversibly interact. Next is the puncture of the target’s membrane as the tail sheath injects the phages genetic material for infection.
Our Focus for Bactail
Phage therapy
Bacteriophages have already been identified to target pathogenic bacteria like Salmonella, Listeria, M. tuberculosis, Pseudomonas or E. coli2. Phage therapy uses bacteriophages, natural bacterial enemies to kill pathogenic bacteria. It originated in Paris, in the early 20th century. The use of these viruses to treat patients infected by bacteria was a safe option at the time but it was soon forgotten when antibiotics were discovered. Nowadays, when the available antibiotics are starting to show weaknesses, bacteriophages are gaining interest to fight against antimicrobial resistance. The concept relies on the use of these bacterial viruses to specifically attach and inject their genetic material into their host, leading to their lysis. Using phage therapy to fight against these infections has already proved beneficial in a number of cases.

In 2019, Tkhilaishvili et al. successfully treated multi drug resistant P. aeruginosa in prosthetic joint infection by using local phage delivery combined with other methods such as antibodies3.

In addition, Kuipers et al. administered a personalized bacteriophage to successfully cure a 58-year-old patient who was suffering from recurrent urinary tract infections caused by carbapenemase-producing K. pneumoniae after a renal transplant4.

However, it is important to note that in both of these reported cases, treating patients using bacteriophages had to sometimes be done in complement to antibiotics. In fact, phages are more effective when used with antibiotics and this phenomenon is called “phage-antibiotic synergy"5. Some antibiotics have been shown to simulate the production of lytic phages. The problem with this method is that it still requires the use of antibiotics and so, it continues to increase AMR.

An alternative to antibiotic resistance would be to harness the characteristics of bacteriophages that make them efficient bacterial predators, and integrate them into a well-studied, controllable chassis.

What we believe
To comfort this idea, studies have confirmed that the expression of the LTFs is sufficient to specifically recognize the target. The first phase of the BacTail project was inspired by an article published in 2019 by Dunne et al. The researchers successfully showed that the recognition and binding of Salmonella cells could be accomplished by only using the LTF of the S16 phage which is the natural predator of Salmonella6. In fact, they developed a novel assay called the “Enzyme Linked Long Tail Fiber Assay” (ELLTA) which was divided into two consecutive steps. Note that the ELLTA is similar to the ELISA but the capture antibody is replaced by the LTF distal tip. In the case of the researchers expressed two adjacent receptor binding proteins (RBP) found at the extremity of the LTF (gene product (gp) 37 and gp38, the adhesin tip) as well as chaperone proteins, in E. coli, the host organism. The RBPs were conjugated at their N-ter extremity either to paramagnetic beads (LTF-MBs) or to horseradish peroxidase (HRPLTF) which are affinity molecules. Their C-ter extremity, composed of the gp38 was able to bind to the Salmonella target’s OmpC. The two developed steps allowed to isolate the Salmonella cells linked to the LTF-MBs and then label them using the HRP-LTF. The experiments conducted by Dunne et al. successfully proved that the unique gp37-gp38 protein of the S16 phage is sufficient to specifically bind the target Salmonella cell.

All in all, LTFs allow the bacteriophage to have a narrow bacterial host range. Meaning that according to the type of LTF expressed, a phage will or will not recognize and infect a certain bacterium.

Main goal(s)

How will we be conducting our proof of concept ?

Which bacteriophage LTF would we be expressing?
The choice of the bacteriophage LTF we would be expressing was quite difficult as not many LTFs have been well characterized and most of them that have, target either pathogenic bacteria or commensal E. coli. We decided to use the T4 coliphage which is a well-studied bacteriophage belonging to the Myoviridae family and known to infect E. coli nonpathogenic bacteria such as E. coli B or K127(Reference sequence: NC_000866.4). Its infection efficiency is one of the highest as it reaches a theoretical value of 1. The LTFs of the T4 phage play a role in the first step of bacterial infection. Indeed, they are known to “walk” on the surface of the host cell in order to bind to specific motifs found in the LPS and OmpC. Furthermore, for more precision on the T4 LTF structure, it was shown that the very distal tip of the LTF codes for an adhesin protein which is structured as a trimer. Mutations in this protein can alter the host specificity of the phage and its ability to bind to the RBP or not2.
Generally, the LTF is composed of gp 34, 35, 36, 37 and 38. However, in the case of the T4 phage, gp38 is not found in the final tail as it codes for a chaperone protein (same as for gp57A). The distal end of the LTF is a trimer where 3 molecules of gp37 are found. Each domain will interact either with OmpC, LPS or both. In 2019, Islam et al. successfully over expressed the gp37(needle sequence), co-expressed with the chaperone proteins, in E. coli and purified it using the N-ter Histidine tag7. The researchers conducted a binding assay by coating a plate with the LTF and putting it in contact with over expressed OmpC. They managed to show that there was no nonspecific binding and that the strength of binding of the LTF needle to the OmpC trimer depended on their concentration.
Which chassis would we be using?
For our chassis, we decided on E. coli as it most known for synthetic biology applications. However, since we were going to make it express the T4 LTF, we had to find a way to make it not self-recognize. After research we found a paper stating that the strain of E. coliK12 ΔOmpC (JW2203) from the Keio Collection8, a collection that includes knockout mutants for all nonessential genes in E. coli K-12, could not be recognized by the T4 LTF2.
Goal for phase 1
Express part of the T4 LTF on the outer membrane of E. coli K12 ΔOmpC and show that it maintains its ability to specifically recognize the target E. coli K12 or B is maintained.
Theoretical verification of thge LTF display

In order to conduct our proof of concept, we needed to know if displaying the T4 bacteriophage’s LTF tip on the surface of E. coli was sufficient to reach the outer membrane of the target bacteria (i.e. long enough according to the elements found on E. coli outer membranes).

We found that the longest known component of a bacterial cell wall is the lipopolysaccharide conjugated with an O antigen which has a length of 49nm7. T4 phage’s LTFs have a length of 145nm9.

Additional characterization on the exact length of the gp37 was not available so we had to proceed by assumptions. Based on the molecular mass of the whole LTF (307 kDa) and the molecular mass of the gp37 product (109 kDa), we can hypothesize that the gp37 protein product has an average length of 51 nm which would mean that our modified E. coli must be able to sense other bacteria thanks to the gp37.

How to achieve it?
A BioBrick with the ability to execute this task, is inserted between a prefix and suffix region of the high copy number plasmid pSB1A3. The major component is the gp37 which codes for the T4 RBP conferring the recognition ability of the target. Gp37 will be directed to the outer membrane of our model organism, E. coli K12 Δ OmpC. This will be achieved thanks to the co-expressed Lpp-OmpA gene. The Lpp-OmpA is an existing part designed by the NCTU-Formosa team in 2015 (Part: BBa_K1694002). It consists of a N-terminal acids of the lipoprotein (Lpp) sequence followed by amino acids of outer membrane protein A (OmpA). By fusing gp37 to the C-ter end of Lpp-OmpA, it can be displayed on the outer membrane of E. coli.
Figure 4: Immunofluorescence to validate the localization of the LTF at the surface of our chassis
Between the Lpp-OmpA and the gp37, a red fluorescent protein (RFP) marker is present allowing with confirmation of LTF expression and localization in the chassis. Additionally, a TEV cleavage site and a His tag are inserted before the RFP marker to verify the expression of the gp37. Finally, LTF assembly is assisted by the chaperone protein gp57A, which helps in the trimerization of gp37, whereas the chaperone protein gp38 is required for proper folding of gp377.
Along the development of our project, and after discussing the construction with some professors, we decided to optimize our BioBrick by removing the RFP.
Figure 5: Our optimized constructs for the expression and localization of the T4 bacteriophage gp37 to the outer membrane of our chassis
How to validate our system?
Instead of using the RFP to locate the LTF on the surface of our chassis, we came up with another, more reliable and simple way to confirm the proper localization of the LTF on the outer membrane of our chassis: immunofluorescence, meaning targeting the His tag with a fluorescent antibody.

Perspectives

Part of the BacTail project also consists in developing a BioBrick design software which is composed of a database with the different bacteriophages associated to their host and the available set of sequences coding for the receptor binding proteins (distal LTF). The goal is to assemble in one simple database, all the different existing and published data. The idea is that from a given target pathogenic bacterium, the software would directly identify the associated LTF and design a key BioBrick to be integrated into our study model. Because this database depends on the existing literature, it will constantly evolve throughout the years as more and more bacteriophages are being discovered and characterized.

The main characteristic of the BacTail project is its modularity and specificity. This means that the engineered bacteria could easily be redesigned to recognize and kill other targets. For safety reasons, our proof of concept was conducted on risk group 1 organisms which are not dangerous for human beings nor the environment. In the future, we would need maintain the designed LTF expression and localization system but replace the gp37 of the T4 phage (as well as the chaperone proteins) by the RBP of another phage which is known to target pathogenic and antibiotic resistant bacteria. Specific bacteriophages have already been identified to target pathogenic bacteria like Salmonella, Listeria, Mycobacterium tuberculosis, Pseudomonas or pathogenic E. coli. For example, Yochi et al., identified the PP01 bacteriophage to specifically target the shiga toxin-producing strain E. coli O157:H7. More specifically, the researchers created a recombinant phage by replacing the distal LTF of the T2 coliphage by the gp38 of the PP01 phage. They showed that the new phage did not infect commensal E. coli anymore, but the pathogenic O157:H7 strain10.

In regard to this first phase, future possibilities include an initial step of in silico designing different LTFs and testing their affinity for specific bacteria using our system. Following this we could target brand new bacteria that could not be specifically targeted before. Another prospect is designing a bacterial detection machine to identify the bacteria causing the infection and couple the system to the software. It would be a fully automized system.
