Phase 2
How to kill the target?
Introduction
Phase 2 is killing the target once the chassis has bound to it. For this we looked into bacteriocins. Bacteriocins are antimicrobial peptides (AMP) naturally synthetized by ribosomes and produced by bacteria to help them survive in a highly competitive polymicrobial environment1. Bacteriocins are mostly synthetized by Gram positive bacteria such as Lactic Acid Bacteria but Gram negatives can also secrete some types of peptides. The first bacteriocin was identified in 19252.
Interesting characteristics about bacteriocins that comforted our choice in using them for our project is that they are considered as non-harmful for human use as they are easily degraded by proteolytic enzymes and can target specific pathogens in contrary to antibiotics so they do not have an impact on the commensal flora, however there are some eventual exceptions1.
Research on bacteriocins to fight against human pathogens exists but most of the time they are combined with drugs. For example, the well-known antibiotic resistant-pathogenic Pseudomonas aeruginosa which, according to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, was responsible for 2 700 in the USA in 2017, is sensitive to the bacteriocin Nisin supplemented with Polymyxin E and Clarithromycin1.
Our Focus for Bactail

As mentioned previously, the proof of concept is conducted using E. coli K-12 ΔOmpC as the chassis and E. coli K-12 or B as the target strain. Meaning that for the proof of concept, we need to choose a bacteriocin that can kill the target but not the host. There are many different types of bacteriocins with various activity spectrums.

Microcins are very small types of bacteriocins whose secretion is not lethal to the producing cell in contrast to colicins which are larger and cause membrane lysis of the producer strain1. Since our final goal is to target antibiotic resistant bacteria, we looked into microcins originally produced by Gram negative bacteria that had already been shown to have an effect on these pathogens while also on the E. coli target of our proof of concept1.

Main goal(s)

The initial goal was to link the physical binding of the LTF to the target with the secretion of AMP. Meaning, the expression of the AMP would be induced once the bacteria bound. We looked into shear stress (since there could be a mechanical signal when the LTF was bound), reengineering two component systems etc. What was difficult was that the LTF was linked to the outer membrane via Lpp-OmpA and that nothing could be expressed upstream of the Lpp without affecting its outer membrane signaling function that we needed.

After months of intense research and brainstorming on this concept we concluded that with the remaining time we had, our initial objective could not be completed: The expression of AMP had to be constitutive and it had to be controlled in a way that it would only affect the target strain once the chassis is nearby. With that in mind, we found a microcin called MccPDI (Proximity Dependent Inhibition) which was first identified in 20093 and requires close physical proximity between the producer and susceptible strains. MccPDI has a narrow spectrum and can kill several strains of E. coli: multidrug resistant E. coli, enterotoxigenic E. coli expressing F5 (K99) and F4 (K88) fimbriae, enterohemorrhagic E. coli O157:H7 and O26, as well as commensal strains such as E. coli K1233. It has also been shown that Shigella is sensitive to MccPDI4. It recognizes specific Outer Membrane Protein F motifs on the target and leads to lethal membrane damage. The MccPDI gene cluster was initially identified in the strain E. coli 25 plasmid4. It is a locus of approximately 4.8kb and is composed of 5 genes which are each necessary for the appropriate expression, activity and secretion of microcin PDI.

Figure 1: Native MccPDI gene cluster found in E. coli 25

The fact that MccPDI targets pathogenic strains as well as Biosafety Level 1 lab strains of E. coli is ideal for our proof of concept as it will allow us to test and validate its activity on E. coli K12 or B without posing any safety risks.

Goal for phase 2

Express the microcin PDI gene cluster in order to achieve secretion of microcin PDI by E. coli and show that its activity against the target cell is maintained.
How to achieve it?

The proximity dependent inhibition characteristic of the microcin we will be using is still under investigation. Since it is currently thought that the inhibition is linked to a concentration of microcin, our goal is to try to respect the concentration initially produced by the E. coli 25 strain for this proof of concept. However, the MccPDI gene cluster originally found in E. coli 25 is not constitutive and is controlled by the EnvZ/OmpR Two Component System (TCS). In fact, knockout mutants of the TCS did not show a PDI+phenotype5. EnvZ is an osmotic sensor which upregulates the phosphorylation of OmpR (a transcription factor) which binds to the promoter region upstream of McpM, McpI and McpA. It was shown that the production of MccPDI was higher in M9 medium compared to LB medium. Furthermore, mcpD and mcpB are under the influence of another transcriptional regulation.


For our proof of concept, we had two options:

- Clone the whole MccPDI cluster as it initially exists with its transcriptional regulation properties

- Only use the coding sequences of each one of the 5 genes and choose our own promoters, ribosome binding sites (RBS) and terminators

Overall the available data on the non-coding sequences found in the original MccPDI gene cluster was not sufficient so we opted for option number 2.


We were lucky to have the opportunity to exchange with Dr. Douglas Call (Regents Professor of Molecular Epidemiology, Associate Director for Research and Graduate Education, Allen School), who has previously published a few articles on MccPDI6. When explaining our strategy to Dr. Call he suggested we should be careful to maintain molar equivalents of McpM and McpI. This gave us the idea to use the Salis Lab RBS Calculator7 to predict the Translation Initiation Rates (TIRs) of the start codons of each of the 5 genes contained in the native cluster.

Figure 2: Comparison of the TIR of our construct with the native MccPDI cluster using the Salis Lab RBS Calculator

After this, the objective is to design our construct with the appropriate promoters and RBS as to respect the native TIRs. We chose to maintain the expression of mcpM, mcpI and mcpA under the control of the same promoter and mcpD and mcpB under the control of another one.

Figure 3: Our constructs for the expression and secretion of McpM

How to validate our system?

In order to validate our system, we will perform an Agar Diffusion Assay. This will confirm the proper secretion of McpM as well as its lytic activity on the target. For this, a sensitive strain is plated (E. coli DH5α or BL21) and disks are inoculated with different bacterial cultures. The positive control is E. coli DH10B containing the native and functional MccPDI gene cluster in a plasmid, kindly provided by Mr. Call. We should see a zone of inhibition around the disk as the microcin is secreted and kills the surrounding cells. This control will be compared to our engineered bacteria: E. coli DH5α containing the reconstructed MccPDI cluster. The negative control is plating the dish with E. coli ΔOmpF which is known to resist against the Microcin’s action thanks to its mutation.

Figure. 4: Agar Diffusion Assay to validate the proper secretion and activity of Microcin PDI
On the left is the positive control, where the plated bacteria are sensitive to Microcin PDI. On the right is the negative control, where the bacteria have a mutation in OmpF and are not affected but Microcin PDI. Figure created with BioRender.com.

To go further in the possible validation methods, we could combine phase 1 and 2 dans see the different inhibition patterns according to the presence or absence of the LTF which allows to localize the engineered bacteria in proximity of its target.

Perspectives

As mentioned in phase 1, we are developing a software that will design the BioBrick with the key LTF gene part according to the target. Since the overall project aims at killing the target, we could look into integrating an AMP database to the software as well if keep this killing method in the future. The goal is to have a tailor-made system that can be easily modified according to the target bacterium. With this in mind, we came across an article published by Geldart et al.8 in 2016 containing preliminary data on a microcin secretion system which could be universal. This means that the secretion system would always be the same (the microcin V system) and only the coding sequence of the AMP would have to be replaced. Furthermore, certain bacteriocins have been identified to fight against biofilms. For example, Microcin S initially produced by probiotic strain E. coli G3/10 manages to inhibit the adherence of enteropathogenic strains of E. coli9. Since antibiotic strains tend to increase their resistance and survive by forming biofilms, it would be interesting to combine the secretion of AMPs to this type of bacteriocin. A limit regarding bacteriocins is that some resistance can occur when there is a continuous exposure. For example, it was shown that a degrading enzyme exists for one of the most famous bacteriocins called Nisin (nisinase).

This issue can be solved by secreting a few AMPs at a time. Indeed, we found a study showing that resistance to individual colicins occurs after 9 hours of treatment but when 5 colicins are combined, no resistance occurs10. Thus, the next step of this phase would be to manage to secrete at least 3 antimicrobial peptides at a time but maintain the specificity of our system. As AMPs are very sensitive to proteolytic enzymes, oral administration often results in peptide degradation. It is necessary to find a way to administer the bacteria to the site of infection which is most of the time found inside the Gastrointestinal Tract. This is the fourth phase of the project which will be discussed further on.
