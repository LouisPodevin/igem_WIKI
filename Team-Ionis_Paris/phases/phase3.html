Introduction
Over the last few decades, synthetic biology has made rapid progress.The use of genetically modified micro-organisms has continuously increased in a wide variety of fields. From agri-food, to the environment or health sector, the related research enabled breakthroughs previously judged as unreachable.
Nevertheless, with these improvements, adequate safety measurements must be drawn to control the escape of new engineered bacteria into the environment.
Thus, while previous studies were focused on methods of cell survival, new ones are putting the spotlight on the different ways to prevent bacterial dissemination, kill-switches. 
Our Focus for Bactail
Problem: releasing transgenic bacteria from the lab UNCERTAINTY
Bacterial interaction with the environment cannot be perfectly predicted.

There are risks of unanticipated survival and reproduction in the environment, with negative ecological effects, but also uncontrolled transfers of genetically engineered DNA to other organisms1.

To limit these risks, researchers have developed kill-switches2. They consist in genetic tools integrated into modified micro-organisms enabling to control their life cycles through different mechanisms. The activation of these safeguards can be done through several artificial systems.

For instance, a kill switch could render the survival, division, and growth of a bacteria directly dependent on the presence of certain chemicals or amino acids3. They would regulate gene expression through the action of an inducible promoter linked to a toxic gene product4. We looked at implementing our bacteria with an appropriate kill-switch system that could be activated following the lysis of the targeted pathogenic bacteria.

Main goal(s)

The final goal of BacTail is to create a therapeutic bacterium to fight against intestinal infections caused by antibiotic resistant bacteria. This means that the product would have to be orally administered to people suffering from infections.

Prevent the bacterium’s dissemination in the environment and the problems linked to it, a kill-switch system has to be inserted into the chassis to destroy it once its mission is fulfilled (i.e. phase 2: killing the pathogen).

The IISER Tirupati 2020 iGEM Team was also working on issues linked to antimicrobial resistance. In a nutshell, their project consists in the destruction of antibiotics found in the feces of farming chicken. Following excretion, antibiotic residues as well as the induced resistant bacteria are transported into the environment. This occurs due to leakage, manure application, and airborne particulate matter and globally contributes to the aggregation of resistance in the environment5. This hazardous issue is frequently encountered in the poultry industry.

With the actual health crisis linked to COVID-19, the IISER Tirupati team did not have the chance to proceed to their experiments in the lab. Thus, a dialogue began about establishing a partnership. This one relied on the design and shipment of the kill switch constructed by the IISER Tirupati team to our lab in France. Our team would then run the associated experiments, with results exploitable by both teams.

The two main parameters that the IISER Tirupati Team had to determine:

The switch system
As their bacteria are planning on being added to tanks containing feces of chicken, they decided to look for an inducible promoter. This way, they could take control of the survival of their bacteria. Through their research, they intended to find an environment-friendly and cheap inducer. There, they took interest in the L-Arabinose operon.
The killing method
The team decided to rely on the death inducing bpDNaseI, an endonuclease capable of hydrolyzing double stranded DNA6. It was concluded that a modified version of the L-Arabinose operon would be used with the bpDNaseI gene instead of those normally expressed.
How to achieve it?
Representation of the constructs expressing DNASE I for the kill-switch and mRFP for the analysis of the expression.

The idea that the IISER Tirupati Team had was to replace the structural genes araB, araA and araD (coding for enzymes catalyzing Arabinose) by the bpDNaseI gene while simplifying the rest of the system. Thus, a minimal pBAD promoter was used7,8.

Derived from the Escherichia coli L-arabinose operon, this version of pBAD contains each of the binding sites for the regulatory protein AraC. These regulatory regions, araI1, araI2, araO1 and araO2, enable the regulation of the expression of pBAD by AraC9. The AraC coding sequence (CDS) was added upstream to pBAD but in the opposite direction and under the control of a medium strength constitutive promoter. The pBAD also retains a catabolite gene activator protein (CAP) binding site where CAP-cAMP complex binds to enhance RNA polymerase binding to the adjacent promoter.

The level of cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) is low when glucose is present and acts as the carbon source10. The inhibition of the cAMP-producing enzyme is a side-effect of the transport of glucose into the bacteria.

Moreover, a 6xHis sequence has been added following the bpDNaseI gene for further investigation. And finally, the whole construct will be cloned into the vector the pSB1A3 high copy number plasmid.

Absence of L-Arabinose / Presence of D-Glucose
As glucose is present, the cAMP producing enzyme is inhibited. The level of cAMP, and therefore of CAP-cAMP complex, is too low to facilitate the binding of the RNA polymerase to pBAD. Also, in absence of L-Arabinose, the AraC dimer binds to operator half-sites O2 and I1. The consequence is a transcription repression through the formation of a DNA looping upstream of the pBAD promoter excluding the RNA polymerase from binding to pBAD. Thus, there is no transcription of the DNASE I and mRFP genes (Table. 1).

Presence of L-Arabinose / Absence of D-Glucose
The presence of L-Arabinose makes it binds to AraC, inducing a conformational change. This enables the binding of the DNA-binding domains of the dimer to adjacent I1 and I2 half-sites. This phenomenon finally gives access to the RNA polymerase to bind pBAD. Additionally, as there is no glucose, the level of CAP-cAMP will be high enough to bind to the CAP binding site in order to facilitate the RNA polymerase binding to pBAD. Thus, the transcription of DNASE I and mRFP occurs (Table. 1).

The presence of L-Arabinose makes it binds to AraC, inducing a conformational change. This enables the binding of the DNA-binding domains of the dimer to adjacent I1 and I2 half-sites. This phenomenon finally gives access for RNA polymerase to bind pBAD. But, as glucose is present, the cAMP producing enzyme is inhibited. This induces a level of cAMP, and therefore of CAP-cAMP complex, which is too low to enhance the binding of the RNA polymerase to pBAD. At the end, the transcription of the DNASE I and mRFP genes still occurs at a noticeable rate but lower than when glucose is absent (Table. 1).

An important point that will have to be kept in mind through the experiments and the analysis of the results is that promoter leakage can occur. Promoter leakage means that the transcription efficiency at the promoter inactive state is much lower than when the promoter is in an active state. In fact, it has been experimentally verified that transcription takes place not only at the active state but also at the inactive state of the promoter 11. This phenomenon is judged as meaningless for certain genes. But, as the associated proteins are endonucleases here, they could kill the host bacteria even if produced in a small amount.

Perspectives
According to the IISER Tirupati iGEM team kill-switch’s way of functioning, the applicability for the BacTail project was a major concern. This BioBrick suggests that after the ingestion of the therapeutic bacteria designed in the BacTail project, the patient should later, take L-arabinose to prevent the dissemination of the modified bacteria.
However, when ingested in its digestible form, L-arabinose is absorbed by the intestinal tract. The problem is that this monosaccharide selectively inhibits intestinal saccharase activity in a noncompetitive manner12. Saccharase is essential for glucose absorption as it turns sucrose into glucose and fructose. Thus, its inhibition in the human gastrointestinal tract does not represent a viable solution.
This partnership with the IISER Tirupati team allowed us to test a type of kill switch that could be useful for our future system. As this consists in a proof of concept, further optimization will be needed to adapt the kill switch to its use in the human body. The existing possibilities consist in changing the inducible promoter or using a repressor.
For example, we could design a promoter which would be activated when the target has died and released certain intracellular elements that would be sensed by our engineered bacterium.
