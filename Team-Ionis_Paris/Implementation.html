Antimicrobial resistance is an urgent healthcare issue. There is an anthropological need embedded in the use of genetically engineered machines.

The difficulty in assessing the impacts of Genetically Modified Organisms (GMO) considers the context in which they are used and the possible alternatives to provide the same solutions by other means.

Our aim is to help face a major healthcare problem: antibiotic resistance. 

How are GMOs being used in the pharmaceutical industry?

Currently, GMOs are used as producer organisms for therapeutic molecules of interest. In France, the commercialization of GMOs directly in food for human consumption has not been authorized.

GMOs used as such as drugs are not authorized. However, some examples in research exist.

Modified lactic acid bacteria have been administered to rats and successfully shown major health benefits as they deliver active molecules to the gastrointestinal tract1.

In April 2019, ZBiotics, a San Francisco-based start-up launched the world’s first GMO probiotic. They modified a strain of Bacillus subtilis to make it produce acetaldehyde dehydrogenase in order to break down acetate. Interestingly, it is regulated as a food and not a drug.

How are GMOs regulated?

As our project is in synthetic biology, it is directly inspired from living organisms.

GMOs: strict, specific, country-based regulations

GMOs are only allowed to be manipulated under restricted conditions and are under the responsibility of the researcher. The latter must ensure that no GMO escapes the strictly regulated domain of application and use. Something that will be controlled if the project is only happening in the laboratory.

In Europe, the main legal frame regarding GMOs is given by the European Union (EU). According to the Directive 2001/18/EC and Regulation 1829/2003, it is prohibited to use or manufacture a GMO without prior specific authorization.

Applications for authorization of GMOs fall into three main categories2:

Contained use

allows GMOs to be used for industrial production or research purposes

Use in deliberate release

in the framework of a marketing authorization (feed or food products, GMO seeds and plants, veterinary medicinal products, gene therapy products, or non-food products such as flowers)

Deliberate release

without placing the product on the market, in the context of experiments such as field trials or biomedical or veterinary research

In France, authorizations for the use of GMOs need to be issued by specific authorities depending on the application domain (Agriculture, Consumption, Health, Research). However, the Ministry of Ecological and Solidarity Transition (MTES) is always consulted. Additionally, no matter thef application, an assessment of the potential risks to human health and the environment is carried out by the High Council for Biotechnology. This council issues an opinion and/or a recommendation, depending on the application3.

Safety aspects to consider

Risks to consider in regards to our therapeutic probiotic6:

1
Must not impact the commensal flora.
2
Make sure that not toxic for human cells (intestinal epithelial cell model).
3
Modification of the intestinal flora: unexpected interactions with bacteria, favor other pathogenic bacteria.
4
Alteration of metabolic functions in the intestinal flora.
5
The survival in the environment and the consequences for public health: the modified bacteria could impact the microbiological environmental equilibrium as well as public health if the soil or animals are contaminated.
6
Potential allergies.

How to limit these risks:

Bacterium should not contain an antibiotic resistance selection marker.

Genes should be inserted in the genomic chromosome and not contained on a plasmid. The location of the genes should be precisely known.

Correct analysis of benefit to risk ratio.

The design of BacTail was carefully thought through for the outside world. The kill switch is one of the most important parameters in our system as the modified bacteria must be precisely controlled to avoid their dissemination in the environment (cf. phase 3).

BacTail: Proof of Concept and next steps

POC: conducted on class 1 organisms at first, for safety reasons, with E. coli as our model organism

1

Target and bind E. coli K12 using the LTF of the T4 bacteriophage 

2

Kill E. coli K12 by secreting antimicrobial peptides

3

Induce the death of our chassis once its mission accomplished

The POC is dedicated to showing that the principle of our system works.

Further system optimization to increase the precision of the therapeutic bacteria’s action.
Fully characterize each step of the POC in the lab (minimal concentration of engineered bacteria needed etc.)

Next POC using pathogenic bacteria as our target.

We have already found in literature that the PP01 bacteriophage is specific to the shiga-toxin producing strain E. coli O157:H7.
That the distal part of the LTF (gp38) is sufficient to specifically bind the pathogenic bacterium and no other ones4 (cf. phase 1 perspectives).

The idea would be to express the gp38 of the PP01 phage at the surface of our chassis to target the shiga toxin producing strain and kill it by secreting the antimicrobial peptides we have used in our project (MccPDI).

Our system would be commercialized as a drug so we would have to follow the traditional standard clinical evaluation phases (patent the system prior to the trails)
Phase 1

"Is it safe? Is it tolerable?"
escalating dose response studies on 10-20 healthy volunteers

Phase 2

"Does it work?"
evaluation of drug effectiveness in 100+ patients with the bacterial infection

Phase 3

"How well does it work? Common side effects?"
evaluation on 1000+ patients

Phase 4

Application request for market approval, surveillance for drug adverse effects over the years.

However, as mentioned by Dr. Chardon during our Human Practices discussions, we could apply for an emergency use authorization if our preliminary results are promising.

Since our project offers a last resort solution to untreatable bacterial infections which are urgent healthcare issues, it could definitely opt for these types of authorizations6.

Fast Track: "facilitate the development, and expedite the review of drugs to treat serious conditions and fill an unmet medical need"

Breakthrough Therapy: "expedite the development and review of drugs which may demonstrate substantial improvement over available therapy"

Since BacTail is tailor made, meaning the bacterium is modified according to the target pathogen, we would have to approve each of the different engineered bacteria.

Furthermore, updates on our software tool (cf. software) would have to be done along the years as more and more bacteriophages are being discovered and their specificity properties are being characterized.
