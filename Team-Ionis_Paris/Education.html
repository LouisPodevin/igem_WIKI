As a responsible biological engineer, it is our duty to take part in opening the dialog between synthetic biology and society, to go beyond the lab.

Participating in the iGEM competition allowed us to acquire knowledge on synthetic biology.
Now, we believe it is our responsibility to teach the public and inform people about this discipline. In order to educate people about synthetic biology, we decided to use several means of communication.

Over the development of our project, our team had the opportunity to host several events and:

1
Communicate on the potential power of synthetic biology to solve numerous problems
2
Raise social awareness about antimicrobial resistance (AMR)
3
Have every single team member participate in multiple events
4
In addition to talking to many scientists and stakeholders about BacTail, in order to consider their advice in our project’s conception, we also presented some scientific concepts to the general public in order to get their insights
5
Assess the positive impact on society of our project
6
Break the lack of communication between what is done in research and the world

A large panel of people were not aware about things scientists were doing in their everyday lives.
These events were perfect occasions to initiate them to concepts around gene editing, applications and perspectives.

Experiments

Experiments

We had the opportunity to participate in the 29th event of the "Fête de la science", organised by the City of Science and Industry, which is the biggest science museum in Europe, located in Paris.
Along with the 4 other Parisian Teams, we hosted a booth during the whole weekend of October the 4th.

60 people reached in two days
For two full days, we hosted discussions on Biology in general, Synthetic Biology, AMR, and BacTail. The most interesting part for the attendees was participating in science experiments.
Mostly families but also teachers and associations actively took part in our experiments.

DNA and genetic material are what our lab team is working with every day.
Because of the importance of DNA, we decided to present a DNA extraction of kiwi experiment to show how little DNA is and to explain what it is.
We explained that first we need to manually break the cells to extract the DNA. This is done using detergent and sodium chloride to create an osmotic stress and disturb the cell membrane.
After filtering the lysate to retrieve the supernatant, we add ethanol at 90° to precipitate the DNA.

In the front, Albane is presenting the kiwi DNA extraction. In the background, Charlotte and Louis were presenting synthetic biology and BacTail. City of Science and Industry, France (09/04/2020)
Renaud presenting BacTail, City of Science and Industry, France (09/04/2020)

We were surprised about how much people have already heard about AMR. We presented our idea which offers a potential solution to AMR and answered several questions, mostly concerning human and environmental safety.
We really pointed out that thanks to synthetic biology and strong regulation, our technology must be as safe as possible in order to put our product on the market.

We also showed the visitors a large-scale model of cells with its components and large-scale model of DNA.
We show them a zoomed view of a butterfly, leaf and fingerprints thanks to a microscope which was really enjoyed by children.
Our goal was to encourage their curiosity about the living.

Why Kahoot?

Kahoot is a free learning platform which enables tests in real time and only requires the use of our mobile phone or laptop.

Throughout lockdown during the pandemic, several teachers from our school tested the comprehension of their classes using Kahoot quizzes.

How to play?

The host prepares all the questions, adds 2 to 4 possible answers and can set a time for each question from 5 seconds to 2 minutes.
The questions are shown on the host’s screen and the students have to pick the right answer(s) directly on their smartphone.
Each player collects a certain amount of points if they pick the correct answer(s).
The faster the players answer, the more points they collect.
Because of the pandemic, we found that Kahoot sessions were an excellent way to instruct people without gathering them in the same room. The format was also ideal to deliver educational content under a modern format as it combined teaching and a dynamic activity.

Collecting Feedback

At the end of the presentations, we gave our team’s e-mail address to the audience in case they wanted to know more about Synthetic Biology and/or BacTail.
Some participants contacted us and we are still in touch with them.

We asked the audience to fill a form at the end of the webinar to know if they thought they learnt something from our webinar, see what that had understood or not, and what could be improved for our future presentations.

Here is some of the data we collected:

Session 1 : October 21st

The first session on Wednesday, 21st of October was dedicated to all the students, teachers and members of the administration of our school Sup'Biotech.
Therefore, 88% of our audience were students in Biotechnology.
We asked all the participants if they had any knowledge on Synthetic Biology before the presentation; 11% of them answered "None", 55% answered "Few" and 34% answered "Lots" (Fig 1).

Fig 1. Participants' knowledge on Synthetic Biology before the presentation

Because most of our public had few or no knowledge in synthetic biology, we wanted to know if the presentation was clear and useful. We were pleased to know that 74% of them had learned a lot on Synthetic Biology thanks to our webinar (Fig. 2).

Fig 2. Participants’ learning degree on Synthetic Biology after the presentation

This pie chart shows that we succeeded in transmitting knowledge to most of our audience.
Next time, maybe we could try to conduct different sessions depending on people’s scientific background and levels in order to improve the percentage.

At the end of the presentation, our team was very curious to know if our presentation has developed any interest to learn more on Synthetic Biology. In fact, 70% of the audience completely developed an interest in this discipline (Fig. 3).

Fig 3. Participants’ development interest in Synthetic Biology after the presentation

This figure indicates that our presentation was very well prepared and it incited people to learn more about Synthetic Biology.
We are thrilled by this data, because this was our first-most goal. Even though some people did not develop an interest in synthetic biology (6%), at least our webinar initiated them to this field.

Fig 4. Kahoot podium of the first session (October 21st)
Session 2 : October 23st

The second session on Friday, 23rd of October was dedicated to a wider public; our families, friends, former colleagues and internship managers, in general; our networks.
Therefore, this session was very heterogeneous in terms of age and field of expertise. In fact, 41% of the audience had 18 to 24 years old, 15% had between 41 and 50 and 15% others had between 51 and 60 years old (Fig. 5).

Fig 5. Age of the audience

As expected for this session, the participants’ field of application are much more diverse than for the first session; 26% are studying or working in Biology/Biotechnology, 13% in health, 13% in finance, 11% in IT services, and 11% in Education (Fig. 6).

Fig 6. Field of application of the participants

Even though, 73% of the audience had no knowledge on Synthetic Biology, 23% understood half of the presentation, and 77% understood everything (Fig.7).

Fig 7. Audience’s understanding degree of the presentation

This pie chart points out the fact that our presentation was clear enough so that the entire audience understood part of the presentation and more than ¾ of them understood everything even if most participants did not have a scientific background. In order to improve this score, we could perhaps go one step further to simplify some of the technical terms we used and popularize the subject.

Fig 8. Kahoot podium of the second session (October 23rd )
Both of these webinars allowed us to reach a wide and diverse audience and to teach them the basics of Synthetic Biology.
Organizing the presentations was a huge amount of work, but the results are higher than expected; we reached, taught and talked to a 200 people about this discipline.
As we know that we are not equal in terms of school education and studies, we are thrilled to have given people the opportunity to learn more about Synthetic Biology.
