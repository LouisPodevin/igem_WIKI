Antibiotic resistance

Overview

From previous success...

Antibiotics are one of the most important scientific progresses in Human History. They allowed to significantly increase Human lifespan1 and health comfort. This powerful solution against pathogenic bacteria, discovered in 1928 by Professor Alexander Fleming helped save thousands of lives and is still curing people now. Numerous families of antibiotics have been discovered and the global market of antibiotics is highly valued at around USD 42,653.89 million in 20182.

Unfortunately, as the living constantly adapts to its environment, so do certain bacteria who encounter antibiotics.
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) is now one of the top priorities for Humanity health.

Nowadays, this resistance is already causing a lot of harm to society. In 2011, the excess healthcare costs of resistant bacterial infections in the US/EU was estimated at $20 / 1.9€ billion5. In a report published by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) in 2019, more than 2.8 million antibiotic-resistant infections occur in the U.S. each year, and as a result, more than 35,000 people have died6.

to current and future ineffectiveness

These resistances to antibiotics are now more or less well understood. Processes like the reduction of intracellular antibiotic concentrations, antibiotic inactivation or target site inactivation due to pressure selection, endanger a great amount of people. Still, even with a lot of effort to search for alternatives to antibiotics, no solution has appeared to be efficient enough.

It is in this context that the BacTail project emerged. This project aims at providing an innovative, last resort solution for AMR. Creating and using therapeutic bacteria may be a powerful solution, but as AMR is a worldwide healthcare issue and affects society, the potential impact it could have must be discussed.

Dr. Blanc-Gruyelle
MÉDIPÔLE HOSPITAL
"Your technology needs to be fast to produce in order to act on sepsis"
Dr. Blanc-Gruyelle is an infectious disease specialist at the Médipôle hospital in Lyon, France. We contacted her because she has a great experience in emergency cases caused by AMR.
Dr. Blanc–Gruyelle seemed enthusiastic about our approach and convinced about our product’s potential action on local infectious.
Dr. Blanc-Gruyelle brought up some issues concerning AMR and drug delivery

Certain human organs cannot be easily accessed by surgery.

For example, we cannot administer liquid into the lungs. So, Dr. Blanc-Gruyelle made us think about our delivery systems for that kind of inaccessible organ. As perspectives, we could investigate nano-biotechnologies for our drug delivery system.

Bacteria and nanotechnology perspectives?

After further literature, we learnt that bacteria could be used as nanomaterial producing organisms7.

A perspective of BacTail would be to use nanotechnology combined to the specific receptor binding protein of the identified bacteriophage, to precisely and locally target the pathogenic bacterium. However, for the first steps of our project in the context of the iGEM competition, we decided to focus on another type of delivery method (probiotics).

Sepsis kills in 48 hours. Our system must act quickly.

Even if our software would provide a fast way to know which therapeutic bacteria to use, we also need to think about their production. Not every hospital has the technologies to produce modified bacteria so, in parallel to our proof of concept, we should work on a service to allow the hospitals to produce bacteria in a fast and safe way.

How would our project be implemented in the real world?

This discussion helped us target the end users of our product as well as the scale at which it would be produced. We believe our system would first, be implemented at a small scale. Dr. Blanc-Gruyelle brought up an important factor: urgency.

We should investigate hospitals as an ideal place to implement the production of the therapeutic bacteria. This will limit the time of transportation and allow to have the diagnosis and therapeutic steps at the same location.

CHINESE CENTER FOR DISEASE CONTROL AND PREVENTION

Dr. Gicquel is Co-director of the San Ming Tuberculosis Project at the CCDC (Chinese Center for Disease Control and Prevention) of Nanshan Shenzhen China and Honorary Professor at the Pasteur Institute.
For 24 years, Dr. Gicquel created and Managed the Mycobacterial Genetics Unit at the Pasteur Institute in Paris. She earned numerous rewards like the title of “Légion d’Honneur”, France's highest order for her work and great contribution to Science.
Dr. Gicquel really enjoyed our concept and seems impressed by our strong scientific background.
Strategy
From one idea to another: signal transduction and autolysins to antimicrobial peptides

Dr. Gicquel really made us rethink our project about our target bacteria destruction system. Up until the month of July, we were thinking about using direct signal transduction once the bacterium binds to its target which could lead to the synthesis of autolysins (like CwlC and CwlHb for example), to provoke the death of the target bacteria. After discussing the different target destruction possibilities with Dr. Gicquel, we concluded that antimicrobial peptides seemed to be most efficient option, if correctly chosen.

How to kill the target ?

After further literature, we learnt that bacteria could be used as nanomaterial producing organisms7.

The destruction of our bacterial target corresponds to phase 2 of our project. By considering Dr. Gicquel’s advice on the use of antimicrobial peptides, we redesigned phase 2, thoroughly searched for the ideal type of peptide and decided to focus on microcin PDI for our proof of concept.

Check phase 2
Safety

Using therapeutic bacteria is also a strong advantage because thanks to synthetic biology, they are definitively more controllable than phages. When phages infect and kill bacteria by a lytic process, numerous phages are released which causes an environmental dispersion problem. There are ways to control the life-cycle of bacteria, which we will further discuss).

Perspectives

Check phase 2
Software

Dr. Debarbieux confirmed that our need of creating a software was justified. A phage only recognizes one specific spectrum of bacterial strains. If we want to treat an entire bacterial species like Salmonella, we will for sure need more than one therapeutic bacterium. Our software would be helpful generate numerous specific therapeutic bacteria.

A software tool to help design the BioBrick for phase 1

Indeed, we want our software to be extremely precise and to have sequences that have been well characterized to specifically target certain strains. When designing our software, we considered the fact that currently, not all these specificity characteristics of bacteriophages have been characterized.

That is why, the software is open source and is built to accept new data from researchers from all over the world who work on bacteriophages.

Philosophy
A long-term view of the impact our project could have on society and in the World
David Ricardo Galeano
Master student in Philosophy of Science, Knowledge and Mind, Sorbonne University (Paris)
"Biology is a living concept while engineering is a mathematical tool"
David Ricardo Galeano studied philosophy for 3 years at the Denominational Faculties of Philosophy and Psychology (IPC), and is currently pursuing a specialization with a Masters in Philosophy of Science, Knowledge and Mind at Sorbonne University.

Reflections on synthetic biology

After we presented our project, David Galeano explained to us that biology is a living concept while engineering science relies on purely mathematical tools.

With our project, we seek an innovative solution combining biology with engineering sciences. So we are confident in saying that schematically, synthetic biology combines mathematical tools of engineering with the living concept of biology. The main difficulty we face is that these mathematical tools seem to render conclusions necessary while the living component belongs to the domain of the contingent.

Our role as scientists is to be conscious of the balance and order established in nature so that, by working with it, we can unfold its full potential. Otherwise, we may be tempted to perform on living beings without trying to understand them.

The work we undertake, with its experimental failures, is a great opportunity which demonstrates that the living being is not a machine and that the primary measure will always be the living organism and not our hypotheses.

Reflections on the societal aspect

David Galeano also made us reflect on the general impact of the release of new technology in our society. As scientists, it is often difficult to anticipate long-term social repercussions.

In our case, the BacTail project seeks to provide an innovative solution to antibiotic resistance. In the event that BacTail becomes accessible to all, we can expect both positive and negative impacts.

Indeed, in many countries, antibiotics are seen as the only and most effective way to combat infections; people are rarely aware of the damage caused by such drugs.

The introduction of BacTail could prompt such awareness. On the other hand, the project could cause economic disruption in places where pharmacology is an important source of income.

However, talking about economic issues amounts to talking about social issues in our current society.

Are people willing to use our product?

After speaking with David Galeano, we took a fresh look at our project and its purpose. More precisely, this discussion prompted us to reflect on aspects outside our scientific field. We decided to launch an online questionnaire to assess people’s willingness to take, or not to take, our innovative solution to antibiotics.

Check survey
Society

As the main idea of our project is to administer our probiotic to human beings, we thought it would be important to gather some input and a general opinion from the end users: society.

In order to have a better understanding on how society views Genetically Modified Organisms, especially in the context of our project, BacTail, we conducted a survey. Thanks to intensive communication, we were able to reach 324 people from all over the Globe (19 countries).

Population sample

From our form, we saw that nearly 70% of the responses come from a population aged between 18-24 and 41-50 years old (Fig. 1). 50,3% come from people who are not actively working in a science field (Fig. 2) and 77,7% of responses come from France (Fig. 3).

Knowledge about bacteria and antibiotics

To make our analysis reliable, we first had to assess the population sample’s global knowledge about bacteria and antibiotics. Thanks to some questions, we saw that nearly 70% of them have a clear idea about what bacteria are (Fig. 4), 95% know that bacteria are living organisms (Fig. 5). We were surprised to see that 95% are conscious that bacteria are not necessarily pathogenic for humans or animals (Fig. 6). Therefore, 82,7% know that we do not have to directly take antibiotics in case of a bacterial infection, it should be prescribed by a practician (Fig. 7). 50% are convinced that antibiotics are only effective against bacteria (Fig. 8).

Thanks to these questions, we saw that a great proportion of the population sample has a good idea of what bacteria are and their link to human health. Still, we decided to continue our public engagement in order to raise more awareness on the link between bacteria, human health, and antibiotic consumption. As well as the fact that antibiotics are only effective against bacteria.

Knowledge about antimicrobial resistance

BacTail aims at tackling antimicrobial resistance. We wanted to see if our population sample has been made aware of this global health issue. Indeed, 94,1% know that bacteria can become resistant to antibiotics (Fig. 9). 63,6% are conscious that one day, if a solution isn’t found, we will not be able to defend ourselves against pathogenic bacteria (Fig. 10).

Because 77,7% of the responses come from France, this overall knowledge can be explain thanks to numerous governmental and social actions conducted in order to raise people’s awareness on bacteria and antimicrobial resistance.

Vision on our technology

Finally, we wanted gather insight on people’s opinions regarding our project. 63,6% of them would be ready to take our therapeutic bacteria in case of infection (Fig. 11). However, this number rises to 87% in case of an imminent death caused by a bacterial infection (Fig. 12).
