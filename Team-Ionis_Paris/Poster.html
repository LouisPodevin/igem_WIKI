Abstract
BacTail : Target, Kill and Self-Destroy, an innovative way to fight against antimicrobial resistance

Since the discovery of antibiotics, their massive and repeated use has led to the appearance of resistant bacteria for which no treatment exists. This is a major healthcare issue to which the IONIS Paris 2020 team is trying to respond. BacTail aims at designing tailor made therapeutic bacteria with three main abilities: Target, Kill and Self-Destroy. The first step is inspired by the specific recognition capabilities of bacteriophages. We will express their binding proteins on the surface of our bacteria. After binding to the pathogenic target, our bacteria will secrete specific antimicrobial peptides to kill it. For environmental and safety purposes, in order to prevent their dissemination, our bacteria is designed with an integrated kill-switch system. Once its mission is completed, it will self-destroy. All in all, BacTail is an innovative way to safely and locally target and kill antibiotic resistant bacteria.

Description
1 - Target
Bind the bacteria of interest

Based on the specificities of bacteriophages
Bacteriophages are the natural predators of bacteria and are not impacted by bacterial resistance: several billions of bacteriophages with different host specificities exist in nature. We exploit their modulable specificity which is given by their receptor binding protein (RBP).

Goal: Express a certain RBP on the outer membrane of the chassis E. coli, for it to specifically recognize and bind to the target.

2 - Kill
Lyse the bacteria of interest

Based on Antimicrobial Peptides (AMP)
Bacterial AMPs, also known as bacteriocins, are peptides with a variable spectrum, which are naturally synthetized by bacteria. They enable survival in highly competitive polymicrobial environments1.

Goal: Express an antimicrobial peptide that will act on the pathogenic target, thus leading to its lysis.

3 - Self-destroy
Program the death of the chassis

Based on a Kill-Switch
It consists in a system preventing the dissemination of the engineered bacteria into the environment once its mission fulfilled2. To do so, a partnership was established with the IISER Tirupati team whose project is also focused on antibiotics. As they could not enter the lab due to Covid-19 and as their designed construct is usable for both of our projects, it was agreed that our team would proceed with their experiments.

Goal: Create an inducible system that will, in presence of L-arabinose3, express the DNA degrading enzyme bpDNaseI4 leading to the chassis’s self-destruction.

Methodology
Phase 1 : Target
Inspired by bacteriophages for their specific recognition ability.

The Long Tail Fiber (LTF) is responsible for the binding of the T4 bacteriophage to the host bacterium1. It is divided into several gene products (gp). The most distal gene product, gp37, is conferring the recognition ability and binding to the pathogen. These receptor binding proteins bind E.coli’s outer membrane protein C’s and Lipopolysaccharides (LPS).

Schematic representation of the most distal region of bacteriophages Long Tail Fibers | The scheme was adapted from Islam MZ, Fokine A, Mahalingam M, Zhang Z, Garcia-Doval C, van Raaij MJ, et al. (2019) Molecular anatomy of the receptor binding module of a bacteriophage long tail fiber. PLoS Pathog 15(12): e1008193.

Figure 2. Optimized constructs for the expression and localization of T4 bacteriophage’s gp37 to the outer membrane of the chassis

  • For specific recognition: gp37 coding for part of the T4 bacteriopage LTF
  • For surface display: Lpp-OmpA consists of N-terminal acids of the lipoprotein (Lpp) sequence followed by amino acids of outer membrane protein A (OmpA).
    • By fusing gp37 to the C-ter end of Lpp-OmpA, it can be displayed on the outer membrane of E. coli.
  • For 3D assembly of gp37:
    • Chaperone protein gp57 helps in its trimerization.
    • Chaperone protein gp38 is required for its proper folding.
  • For validation: a TEV cleavage site and a His tag are inserted.

Methodology
Phase 3: Self-destroy

Risks linked to the release of transgenic bacteria:

  • Risks of unanticipated survival and reproduction in the environment
  • Risks of uncontrolled transfers of genetically engineered DNA to other organisms1

Solution: Kill-Switch

  • Genetic tool enabling to control the life cycles of genetically engineered bacteria
  • Used to prevent its dissemination in the environment2

International Partnership IONIS PARIS – IISER TIRUPATI:

  • Indian team without any access to the lab due to the global pandemic
  • Both projects focusing on antibiotic problematics and including a kill-switch
  • They design the BioBricks and we proceed to the experiments

Design:

  • Switching system: Based on the L-arabinose operon3
  • Killing system: Endonuclease cleaving dsDNA (DNASE I gene)4

How to validate this system?

  • DNASE I activation via addition of L-arabinose: bpDNaseI production → Cell destruction and reducing OD600
  • mRFP expression analysis: determination of the optimal L-arabinose concentration for the expression of the gene following the composite part BBa-K3519010 via fluorescence spectroscopy

Target | Bind the bacteria of interest

The aim is to detect if the receptor binding protein gp37 is expressed and directed to the outer membrane thanks to the co-expressed Lpp-OmpA genes. The fragments are assembled and transformed. Then, an immunofluorescent assay is performed to detect the presence of gp37 via the fused His-tag.

  • One sample seems to emit a signal of ≈43000 :
    • The highest sample of Ab(1/100)
Interpretation & conclusion:
  • The highest signal is not significant enough
  • Similar signals between samples and negative controls suggest unspecific bindings of the antibody
  • Assay should be retaken with optimized blocking conditions (eg: higher concentration of BSA) to avoid unspecific binding
  • Other assays should be conducted to confirm prior receptor binding protein expression in and outside the bacteria for example via Western Blot.
Results 2/3 – Phase 2: Kill
Kill | Lysis of the bacteria of interest

The goal is to express an antimicrobial peptide which will be secreted by the chassis and act on the target bacteria. For this, the production and secretion of the microcin PDI is needed. Following the plasmid assembly, we need to validate the production of McpM:

1.
Extraction and purification of protein from the cellular culture via Ni2+ chromatography column and further revelation via SDS-PAGE and Coomassie Staining Technique.

Figure 1. SDS-PAGE and Coomassie staining of the purified cell lysate, flow through and total lysate to validate the presence of McpM | Well 1: Ladder, Well 2: First elution of the chromatography column, Well 3: Second elution, Well 4: Third elution, Well 5: Flow through, Well 6: Total lysate.

  • Procedure: Total cell lysate purification of the bacteria that were transformed with the assembled plasmid (carrying the MccPDI gene cluster), migration by SDS-Page and revelation by Coomassie blue
  • Goal: First validation for the production of McpM
  • Observations: Band present with the appropriate molecular weight (8.2kDa)
  • Discussion: A lot of other bands are present in the same lane which indicates that the chromatography was not 100% efficient
To completely validate the presence of McpM in the purified cell lysate, a Western blot and immunodetection using an anti-His antibody should be performed.
2.
Further Confirmation: Agar Diffusion Assay
  • Procedure: The target strain was put in contact with the microcin producing one
  • Goal: Check the presence of an inhibition zone
  • Observations: Clear inhibition zone visible around the microcin producing strain. This indicates that the engineered bacteria manages to correctly secrete microcin PDI and that it retains its activity against the target strain. It is fully functional.

Results 3/3 – Phase 3: Self-destroy
Self-Destruction | Dissemination prevention

The goal is to develop of Kill-Switch system that will activate only in presence of L-arabinose. Then, the expression of the DNASE I gene is induced, leading to the death of the engineered bacteria. This way, we can control the life-cycle of the bacteria.

1.
The constructs are assembled via BioBrick Assembly Method. Four verification assays are realized to determine if a clone has a well-constructed recombinant plasmid

  • Clone N°1 from the pSB4K5 + mRFP plate

Software Tool
To generate a BioBrick giving the ability to recognise a target bacteria

This software relies on the first phase of our project consisting in designing a BioBrick to express bacteriophages Long Tail Fibers (LTF), which are receptor binding proteins (RBP) that bind the target. This will provide the chassis the same specific recognition abilities as bacteriophages. During our project we limited our proof of concept to one target bacterium, Escherichia coli K12, and used the corresponding RBP, namely the gp37 of the T4 bacteriophage LTF.

To make the system of BacTail more exploitable, we created a software tool to generate BioBricks according to desired target bacteria.

How does it work?

Figure 1. Software functionning

Figure 2. Screen capture of a result example from the software tool

Thus, this open-source software aims at creating a fully tailor-made BioBrick (Figure 2). The final BioBrick design incorporates the genes necessary for specific recognition of the target bacteria of interest and this comes out as a ready to use insert.

Open source? But why?

  • Totally expandable software
  • Database depending on the availability of characterized LTF of bacteriophage in literature
  • Sequences of new specific RBP can be easily added by any user into the database
  • Users help each other by extending the range of targets

Helpful for future iGEM teams working on:

  • Protein expression on the outer membrane
  • Phage specificity
  • AMR
