Introduction

Thanks to the sequencing revolution, a vast amount of data is now available worldwide on different databases.

As an example, the GenBank number of bases has doubled approximately every 18 months from 1985 to 20201.

This is a great opportunity for all the science community to easily get data but there is a major problem to this breakthrough, the annotation of sequenced genomes. Today, most annotations are done automatically thanks to different programs, but errors are still prevalent2. To correct these errors, manual curation is the only way to correct them, but it is time consuming, not standardized and subjective to the person doing the procedure3.

There is a global effort to find a better way to annotate this vast and growing amount of data but still, researchers struggle to find the right database for their needs4.

That is why our team has created an open source software where future iGEM Teams and researchers can find annotated sequences of different phages which could be used to create therapeutic bacteria. This database is also a collaborative work to continue throughout the years as new data can be added to increase the database.

BacTail is a tailor-made approach to specifically recognize, bind and lyse antibiotic resistant pathogens thanks to genetically engineered bacteria.

Link for the Project Design

The first phase of our project relies on designing BioBricks that will be integrated into a plasmid for the expression of Long Tail Fibers, which are target receptor binding proteins, on the surface of our bacterium. This will provide to the chassis the same specific recognition abilities as bacteriophages.

The process of creating the plasmid will vary depending on the bacterial strain that we aim to target. Check phase 1

It was very difficult and time-consuming for our team to find the precisely characterized LTF DNA sequences to design the BioBricks.
Imagine the time we would save if a database with all the characterized Receptor Binding Proteins on the distal end of the LTFs existed?
This would have saved weeks of work and we could have had the time to try different combinations of LTF on the surface of our chassis to target the corresponding bacteria.
It came to our minds that we could facilitate the work of next iGEM teams, but also of any other R&D teams, by creating a reliable Software1.

How does the search page work?
This page is based on a form that the user fills step by step:
1
Select the desired target bacterium. There, the associated bacteriophage LTF and required chaperone sequences
will be retrieved from the database. There can be zero to two chaperone sequences according to the bacteriophage.
2
Select one of the different suggested promoters2, Ribosome Binding Sites (RBS)3 and terminators4 to fully customize
the final BioBrick. These are existing parts directly imported form the iGEM Registry of Standard Biological Parts.
3
The BioBrick is formed by successively adding the DNA sequences of each element. To display the information in a
simpler way, we used images to symbolize the promoters, RBS, Coding Sequences (CDS) and terminators. When
selected, each part presents a button that, when clicked on, reveals the corresponding DNA sequence. This safety
feature ensures a full control on the plasmid sequence.
For the software programming, we used secure sql requests with mySqli to find the appropriate DNA sequences from the database. Then, they are concatenated to form a fully formed BioBrick sequence.

Part Documenting
Our project, BacTail, aims at fighting against antimicrobial resistance using an engineered bacterium which will be able to bind to target bacteria and lyse them using specific antimicrobial peptides.


When searching the registry for documented parts on antimicrobial peptides (AMP), we found BBa_K1931014, containing the coding sequence for Microcin E492, which did not contain much information. Surprisingly, we had also found this AMP while doing the initial literature work on our project. Our team believes in the power of antimicrobial peptides and so, we decided it was our duty to add as much information as possible learnt from the literature, to this part.
Screenshot of Part BBa_K1931014 before adding our documentation
How did we contribute?
1
Added information learnt from the literature.
2
Added an experiment to determine the molecular mass of microcin E492 from the literature.
3
Added an experiment to show the ability of microcin to induce apoptosis and necrosis.
4
Annotated the part with the correct sequences.
