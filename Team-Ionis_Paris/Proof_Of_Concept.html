Proof of concept

Bactail is a tailor-made and easy to use system designed to fight safely and locally against antimicrobial resistance (AMR).

Tailor-made & easy to use

The main characteristic of the BacTail project is its modularity and specificity. This means that the engineered bacteria could easily be redesigned to recognize and kill other targets. For safety reasons, our proof of concept was conducted on risk group 1 organisms which are not dangerous for human beings nor the environment. However, the recognition and the lysis step (Design Phase I, Design Phase II) of our project can easily be used individually and eventually be re-adapted for other application areas.

To encourage the application of our tailor made system, a software tool was created (Software). This software aims at generating the BioBrick that gives the ability to recognise a target bacterium of your choice. Eventually, this was thought as a collaborative software, because users are able to add new targets or knowledge to the database as well.

Safe & locally

Our engineered bacteria that has the ability to recognize and bind to bacteria of interest, and kill it, was carefully thought through. Thanks to a self-deletion system, called a Kill Switch, the genetically modified bacteria is programmed to die once it has accomplished its mission. Thus, no dissemination into the environment need be feared (Design Phase III).

BacTail is raising awareness for a major health problem

Our project deals with a major health issue: antimicrobial resistance (AMR). This resistance is already causing a lot of harm to society and concerns everyone. Thus, we were devoted to participate or even organize our own events to find as many occasions as possible to educate people regarding this sanitary crisis (see Public engagement and education).

Eventually, our goal was to talk about AMR and BacTail with a panel of experts. Considering their point of view, to help shape our project (see Human practices).

Experimental validation

Phase 2 | The goal is to express an antimicrobial peptide which will be secreted by our chassis and act on the bacteria of interest. For this, the production and secretion of the microcin PDI is needed (design phase II).

Once the plasmid assembled we needed to validate the production of McpM, the protein has to be extracted and purified from the cellular culture via Ni2+ chromatography column and further revelation via SDS-Page and Coomassie staining technique.

Figure 1 : SDS-PAGE and Coomassie staining of the purified cell lysate, flow through and total lysate to validate the presence of McpM | Well 1: Ladder, Well 2: Elution tube N°1, Well 3: Elution tube N°2, Well 4: Elution tube N°3, Well 5: Flow through, Well 6: Total lysate.

Total cell lysate purification of the bacteria that were transformed with the assembled plasmid (carrying the MccPDI gene cluster: 5 genes necessary for the appropriate expression, activation and secretion of microcin PDI), migration by SDS-Page and revelation by Coomassie blue, is a first validation for the production of McpM as a band was observed at the appropriate molecular weight (8.2kDa).

Figure 2 : Results of the agar diffusion assay | Plate on the left side: Assay N°1 and plate on the right: Assay n°2; For both plates: E.coli DH5 alpha transformed with pSB1A3 containing RFP bacterial lawn; Disc 1: E.coli DH5 alpha colonies from plasmid 31; Disc 2: E.coli DH5 alpha colonies from plasmid 39; Disc 3: E.coli DH5 alpha colonies from plasmid 31; Disc 4: E.coli DH5 alpha colonies from plasmid 39; Disc 5: Positive control - E.coli DH10B strain containing the native MccpDI cluster; Disc 6: Negative control- LB medium; Disc 7: Positive control - E.coli DH10B strain containing the native MccpDI cluster.

To further confirm this observation, an agar diffusion assay was performed, where the target strain was put in contact with the microcin producing one. The goal was to see if a zone of inhibition was visible or not.

A zone of inhibition around the microcin producing strain was clear. This indicates that our engineered bacteria manage to correctly secrete the microcin PDI and that it retains its activity against the target strain. It is fully functional.

Implementing Phase 1 and 2 BacTail in the real world

In the lab, we worked on E. coli K12 to model our target bacterium. In the real world, our system aims at fighting against pathogenic bacteria and antibiotic resistant strains. That is why the future steps of phase 2 is testing our antimicrobial peptide (MccPDI) which has an effect against E. coli O157:H7 for example, by performing another agar diffusion assay in the appropriate biosafety laboratory and safety measures.

Succeeding to accomplish phase 1 and phase 2 in parallel in the same chassis would be ideal for our project. Indeed, once phase 1 has been validated, the goal is to express the LTF at the surface of antimicrobial producing bacteria and show that it increases the efficiency of our chassis as it allows it to approach the target and kill it quicker.

If we were to accomplish this in a biosafety level 1 laboratory on E. coli K12, we could go to the next step and test our system with a real pathogenic target, E. coli O157:H7 (as mentioned above), with appropriate safety measures. For this, we would only have to replace the gp37 sequence of the T4 phage in our chassis, with the gp38 sequence of the PP01 phage, which is already characterized to specifically bind to the shiga-toxin producing E. coli strain and no other commensal ones.

All in all, these tests would validate the modularity of our system, the fact that it is tailor-made and easy to use to fight pathogens in a safe and controlled way.
