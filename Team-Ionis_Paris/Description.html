Project background

The 20th century is marked by a major healthcare breakthrough. Indeed, in 1928, Alexander Fleming discovered penicillin. This antibiotic (ATB) made a big difference in the first half of the past century as many bacterial infections were successfully treated thanks to it. Penicillin helped reduce the number of deaths during WWII. Following this, more ATBs were found, and their efficiency contributed to the greatest medical advances of the past century.

Nevertheless, the overconsumption and misuse of ATBs nowadays is a major problem. Indeed, the World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that in some countries, more than half of the antibiotics are misused1. This can lead to deadly consequences as natural selection is favoring the evolution of resistant bacteria. Indeed, ATBs induce a selective pressure on bacteria which have natural processes which encourage their resistance2. It happens at a genetic level through mutations. And, as bacteria have the capability of transferring their genetic material between each other, these genetic advantages are spread throughout bacteria.

Dangerous and untreatable pathogens are slowly emerging such as methicillin-resistant strains. These pathogens cause multidrug-resistant infections, representing a growing healthcare concern in the whole World. Some infections include pneumonia, tuberculosis, blood poisoning or gonorrhea. With antibiotic resistance, these infections can now potentially kill. According to the WHO, we are close to reaching the post-antibiotic era.

In the next few years, these numbers will highly increase because, if a viable solution isnâ€™t found before 2050, Antimicrobial Resistance (AMR) could cause 10 million deaths per year5. On an economic point of view, AMR costs $350 million per year to the French healthcare system6.

The project: Bactail
Thus, the iGEM Ionis Paris Team is proud to present BacTail, a modulable, innovative and controlled system to fight against AMR.
BacTail offers an innovative alternative to antibiotics. We are developing a tailor-made engineered therapeutic bacterium inspired by bacteriophages. We believe in the power of synthetic biology to tackle deadly infections which are major healthcare issues. The project is divided into four consecutive phases.
Phase 1
The first part of our project is the specific recognition of a target bacterium. For this, we are interested in the ability that bacteriophages have to constantly adapt to their target. Indeed, bacteriophages are the natural predators of bacteria and are not impacted by bacterial resistance: thousands of bacteriophages with different host specificities exist in nature. We will be exploiting their modulable specificity which is given by their receptor binding protein (RBP) found at the extremity of phages. Thus, for the first phase, the goal is to express a certain RBP on the outer membrane of our chassis, Escherichia coli, for it to specifically recognize and bind to the target.
Link
Phase 2
The second part is the death of the target pathogenic bacteria. For this we have looked into antimicrobial peptides (AMP), also known as bacteriocins which are already used in agrifood. Bacteriocins are peptides with a variable spectrum, which are naturally synthetized by bacteria. They allow them to survive in highly competitive polymicrobial environments7. The goal is to express an antimicrobial peptide which will be secreted by E. coli and act on the pathogenic target, thus leading to its lysis.
Link
Phase 3
The third part is the kill switch, a system enabling to prevent the dissemination of the engineered bacteria into the environment once its mission fulfilled. To do so, a collaboration was established with the IISER Tirupati iGEM Team whose project is also focused on antibiotics. As they could not manipulate due to Covid-19 and as their designed construct is usable for both of our projects, it was agreed that our team would proceed to their experiments. Through the production of a DNA degrading enzyme, called bpDNaseI, induced by arabinose, the destruction of the chassis is ensured.
Link
Phase 4
The fourth and final step of our project concerns the galenic form of our product. There, we established the specific requirements to determine the best way to store and administer our engineered bacteria as a probiotic. As it must be able to survive to the acidity of the stomach and retain its specificities in the time, it was decided that the perspectives of our project would be to freeze-dry our engineered bacteria so it can be stored in plant-based hard-shell capsules.
